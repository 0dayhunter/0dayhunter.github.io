My OSCP Journey

Created2026-07-15|notes

After years of grinding Active Directory boxes on Vulnlab and Hack The Box, I finally decided to sit the exam everyone in this space eventually circles back to: the OSCP. This is the honest version of my journey — what I did, what I'd skip, and what actually moved the needle. No gatekeeping, no "just try harder."

Why I went for it

I wasn't chasing the cert for the letters. I wanted the thing OSCP forces on you: a repeatable methodology under pressure. It's easy to feel competent when you have unlimited time and a Discord to ask in. The exam strips that away — a clock, a fresh network, and only your notes. That's the skill I actually wanted to sharpen.

How I prepared

My prep broke down into three buckets, in order of how much they helped:

  • Proving Grounds Practice — the closest thing to exam-flavored boxes. I worked through the TJnull list and treated every box as if it were the real exam: no looking things up until I'd genuinely exhausted my own ideas.
  • The PWK labs — underrated for one reason: pivoting. Rolling one foothold into a whole network taught me more about enumeration discipline than any single box.
  • Hack The Box & Vulnlab — where my AD instincts came from. The exam's AD set felt familiar precisely because I'd already burned myself on Kerberos delegation and ACL abuse a hundred times.

The single biggest jump in my success rate came the day I stopped collecting tools and started writing my own methodology checklist. Tools are cheap. A process you trust at 3 a.m. is not.

Note-taking is the real exam

I can't overstate this: the report is graded, not your ego. I kept a running template for every box with these sections, and I filled them in as I worked, not afterward:

# Target: 10.10.x.x
## Recon
- nmap -sC -sV -oN nmap/initial 10.10.x.x
## Foothold
- service > vuln > exploit > proof
## PrivEsc
- enum > vector > escalation > proof
## Loot / creds

Every command, every screenshot, every credential — captured the moment it happened. When the adrenaline hits during the exam, you will not remember which host you pulled that hash from. Your notes will.

Exam day

The format is 24 hours of hands-on, then a second window to write the report. My personal rules for the day:

  • Enumerate wider before you go deeper. I front-loaded scanning across everything, then picked the target with the most obvious surface first for an early win and a confidence boost.
  • Take the break. I stepped away, ate a real meal, and slept a few hours. The box I was stuck on cracked in ten minutes when I came back with fresh eyes.
  • Screenshot the flag and the command that produced it. A proof with no context is a contested point.
  • Watch the clock, not the ego. If a path costs 90 minutes with nothing to show, park it and rotate. Come back later.

What I'd tell my past self

Three things I wish I'd internalized sooner:

  • Enumeration is 80% of the work. Almost every time I was stuck, it was because I'd missed something, not because I lacked an exploit.
  • Simple before clever. Default creds, password reuse, and misconfigurations beat exotic CVEs on the exam more often than you'd think.
  • Consistency > intensity. A box a day for months did more than any weekend marathon.

If you're on this road right now: trust the reps. The exam isn't looking for a genius — it's looking for someone with a calm, repeatable process who documents everything. Build that, and the letters take care of themselves.

Know the 2025/2026 format (OSCP+)

If you're reading older community experiences, calibrate them against the current exam. Since the OSCP+ update, a few things are worth internalizing before you book:

  • No more bonus points. Every point has to be earned inside the exam environment — there's no cushion from lab or coursework submissions anymore. Plan to actually clear machines, not lean on a head start.
  • Active Directory is "assumed compromise." You start with a standard domain user and work toward full domain compromise. The good news: partial AD points now exist, so a single stuck step no longer zeroes the whole set — but the set is still chained, and a missed pivot can wall off everything behind it.
  • Two badges, one pass. Clearing it grants OSCP (lifetime) and OSCP+ (renewable). Same exam; don't overthink the naming.

The practical takeaway from the format shift: drill Active Directory until pivoting is muscle memory. The chained AD scenario is where most of the recent pass/fail stories turn.

What the community taught me (late 2025)

I read through a stack of OSCP experiences shared by the community who sat the exam through the back half of 2025 — the "passed on my second try" ones especially. Timelines ranged from a focused ~60 days to a steady six months, but the same threads kept surfacing, and they matched my own experience almost point for point:

  • Consistency beats cramming — universally. Nobody who binged for two weekends passed. The people who made it did a little every day for months. This was the single most repeated lesson.
  • Time-box your practice like the real thing. A common drill: 2-hour timer on standalone boxes, ~4 hours on an AD set. If you can't finish in the window, that's the gap to close before exam day, not during it.
  • Volume of boxes matters. Most successful candidates worked through the TJnull list plus dozens of machines across Proving Grounds, Hack The Box and Vulnlab. There's no magic number, but "a lot" is the honest answer.
  • Failure is part of the arc. A striking number of the best posts are "failed once, adapted, passed." The first attempt taught them where their methodology leaked; the second was almost calm by comparison. If you fail, you are exactly on schedule.
  • The wall is mental, not technical. Stress, fatigue, and tunnel-vision fail more candidates than any missing exploit. Sleep, eat, and step away — the recurring "it cracked after my break" story is real.

The meta-lesson across every post I read: the exam doesn't reward the flashiest hacker. It rewards the one with a boring, repeatable process who documents relentlessly and keeps a cool head. Build that person.

A note on playing it straight

One thing I want to be explicit about, because it matters: everything here is general prep advice, mindset, and publicly known exam structure — nothing more. I'm not sharing exam machines, flags, solutions, scripts, or screenshots from any OffSec material, and you shouldn't either. That's not just about staying on the right side of OffSec's rules (though it is a hard requirement) — it's that the value of this cert is that everyone earns it the same honest way.

You can absolutely blog your journey. Talk about your struggles, your mindset, the methodologies and public resources that helped, sanitized examples clearly labeled as such, even a screenshot of your progress dashboard. Just keep the actual course and exam content where it belongs. Play it straight and the achievement means something.
